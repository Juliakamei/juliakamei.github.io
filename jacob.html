Jacob I Bernoulli (1654-1705)


Although there was no family tradition prior to him, Jacob Bernoulli (1654- 1705), Nicholas Bernoulli’s oldest son, was the first in the family to be recognised as a strong influential mathematician. His parents forced him to study philosophy and theology, which he despised, but he earned a theology degree in 1676. Bernoulli moved to Geneva after finishing his theology degree to work as a tutor. He then went to France and spent two years studying with Descartes’ followers. Bernoulli visited the Netherlands and then England in 1681, where he met several mathematicians, including the famous scientists Robert Hooke (1635-1703) and Robert Boyle (1627-1691). As a result of his travels, Bernoulli established correspondence with many mathematicians, which he maintained for many years. He was so fascinated by mathematics and astronomy that he pursued these fields despite his father’s opposition. Since 1683, Jacob Bernoulli has taught mechanics at the University of Basel in Switzerland. Despite having a theology degree and being offered a position in the Church, he declined. Bernoulli’s true passion was mathematics and theoretical physics, and he taught and researched in these fields.

In a paper published in Acta Eruditorum in May 1690 (the oldest scientific journal, which began publishing in 1682 under the supervision of Gottfried Leibniz (1646-1716), Jacob Bernoulli demonstrated that determining the isochrone is equivalent to solving a first-order nonlinear differential equation. The isochrone, also known as the curve of constant descent, is the curve along which a particle will descend under gravity from any point on the curve to the bottom in exactly the same time regardless of where it begins. Huygens investigated it in 1687, and Leibniz in 1689. Jacob Bernoulli was also the first mathematician to use the term "integral calculus" in this paper. Previously, Leibniz referred to it as the "Calculus summatorium".

Some of his great contribution to mathematics are:

  • Bernoulli solved the differential equation after discovering it through what is now known as separation of variables.
  • Jacob Bernoulli’s paper from 1690 is significant in the history of calculus because it is the first time the term "integral" appears with its integration meaning. In 1696, Jacob Bernoulli solved the equation now called "the Bernoulli equation":$$y′ = p(x)y + q(x)y′′$$
  • Jacob's finest concepts: his theory of permutations and combinations; the so-called Bernoulli numbers, from which he derived the exponential series; his treatment of mathematical and moral predictability; and the subject of probability, which includes what is now known as the Bernoulli law of large numbers, which is fundamental to all modern sampling theory.
