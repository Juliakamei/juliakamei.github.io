Johann I Bernoulli (1667-1748)


Jacob Bernoulli’s younger brother was Johann Bernoulli. He was a brilliant mathematician who made significant contributions to the field of calculus. Johann’s father, Nicholas, once again provided him with a well-rounded education. that he might one day join the family business Johann, on the other hand, had no desire to work in the spice trade and enrolled in the University of Basel to study medicine in 1683. He requested that his brother, Jacob, teach him mathematics. Jacob, who was twelve years Johann’s senior, was a mathematics professor at the University of Basel at the time. Johann studied Leibniz’s calculus papers with Jacob’s assistance until he became proficient, and his mathematical prowess soon matched that of his brother.

Calculus was a difficult subject to grasp at the time. The Bernoulli brothers were among the few European mathematicians who understood calculus. Jacob had no trouble teaching his younger brother at first. He recognised his brother’s mathematical abilities and offered to work with Johann. However, the two brothers’ collaboration quickly devolved into heated arguments. Johann’s ego grew larger as time passed. He began to boast about his accomplishments while disparaging his brother. Jacob was so enraged that he made crude remarks about Johann’s abilities, referring to him as a student who simply repeated what the teacher taught him, a parrot. Jacob and Johann exchanged insulting comments about each other in the academic community, earning them a notorious reputation for their family unity.

Johann was a brilliant mathematician despite his family problems. Johann was possibly a more productive scientist than Jacob. He studied differential equation theory and discovered fundamental principles of mechanics and optical laws. He used calculus to solve problems concerning Newton’s laws of gravitation that Newton had failed to solve. Johann also contributed significantly to the study of the equation: $$ y= x^x $$

He discovered the Bernoulli series and made advances in navigation theory and ship sailing. He is also famous for his incredible letter writing (over 2500 messages) and tutoring of another great mathematician, Leonhard Euler.

Johann proposed the brachistrochrone problem, which is the fastest descent curve of a particle sliding from one point to another under the influence of gravity. Johann challenged the "savviest mathematicians from all over the world" to solve the problem in six months. There were five correct answers. Four of these came from well-known mathematicians, including L’Hôpital, Leibniz, Jacob, and Johann himself, and one from an anonymous mathematician. However, based on the method used to solve the problem, experts recognised that the anonymous solver was none other than Sir Isaac Newton, and Johann commented on it "Ex ungue Leonem" (Tell a lion by its claw). Newton later admitted that he solved the problem by obsessively thinking about it for 12 hours. The solution to this problem is an inverted cycloid fragment. A cycloid is the curve traced by a fixed point on the perimeter of a straight-line-rolling wheel.

Johann’s solution to the brachistochrone problem was revolutionary in that it laid the groundwork for a new branch of calculus known as calculus of variations. Calculus of variations, a generalisation of calculus, deals with extremizing functionals as opposed to functions in ordinary calculus. Huygens discovered in 1673, twenty-three years after the brachistotone problem, that the cycloid is also the solution to the "tautochrone problem." The "tautochrone problem" requires finding a curve that allows a particle moving under gravity to reach a given point on the curve in equal time regardless of its initial position on the curve.

Knowing that the cycloid solves both the "brachistotone problem" and the "tautochrone problem," Johann was moved to write: "But you will be petrified with astonishment when I say that exactly this same cycloid, the tautochrone of Huygens, is the brachistotone we are seeking."
