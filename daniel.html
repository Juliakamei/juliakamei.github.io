Daniel I Bernoulli (1700-1782)


Johann Bernoulli’s second son was Daniel Bernoulli. On February 8, 1700, he was born in Groningen, Netherlands. Johann Bernoulli, like his father, did not want his son to pursue a career in mathematics. Johann Bernoulli attempted to persuade Daniel that mathematics is unprofitable and attempted to place Daniel in an apprenticeship with a merchant, but Daniel refused. Daniel began studying logic and philosophy at the University of Basel when he was thirteen, and he graduated two years later with a bachelor’s degree. Bernoulli went to Venice to practise medicine in the early 1720s. He also planned to continue his studies at the University of Padua, but he became too ill to do so. Instead, he concentrated on mathematics, publishing his first book, "Mathematical Exercises," in 1724. Bernoulli also designed an hourglass for use at sea within a year. The sand trickle had to be consistent, no matter how violently the ship moved, even in a storm. At the age of twenty-five, he entered this work in the Paris Academy’s annual competition and won first prize.

As a result of this award and the fame brought about by "Mathematical Exercises," Bernoulli was invited to accept the chair of mathematics at the Imperial Academy in St. Petersburg by Catherine I of Russia, Peter the Great’s widow. At the same time, his brother, Nicolaus, was offered a chair in mathematics at St. Petersburg.

The brothers considered a game in which a coin is tossed "n" times until the first "heads" appears. If you get a head for the first time on the nth toss, you get 2n dollars. What amount of money would you be willing to pay to play this game? Despite the fact that the expected value of the payoff is infinite as:

E=12.2+14.4+18.8+...

= 1+1+1+...

= ∞

Of course, no one appears to be willing to pay this amount to participate in this game. So, Daniel Bernoulli’s solution to the paradox was to assume that utility is strictly concave in the payoff rather than linear, e.g., ln (x). What particular payoff, c, would an individual with such a utility function consider to be the St. Petersburg lottery? This c would have to meet the following requirements:

lnc=∑ni=112iln2i

=∑ni=112iln2

=2ln2

As a result, c = 4 is a rather modest value to assign to the infinite expectation. The St. Petersburg paradox arose as a result of this.

Nicolaus died just a few months after the brothers arrived in St. Petersburg, so Johann Bernoulli arranged for his best student and Daniel Bernoulli’s childhood friend, Leonhard Euler, to come and help him. Bernoulli’s most productive years would be the next few years. He defined the simple nodes and frequencies of a system’s oscillation while studying vibrating systems, and demonstrated that the strings of musical instruments move in an infinite number of harmonic vibrations all along the string.

Between 1725 and 1749, Daniel received ten prizes from the Paris Academy of Sciences for his work in astronomy, gravity, tides, magnetism, ocean currents, and ship behaviour. He also made significant contributions in probability.

Johann and Daniel’s relationship strained when they both competed for the Grand Prix in 1734. Paris Academy Prize, a mathematical and physical sciences school. They both prevailed, and the prospect of Johann was enraged because he shared the prize with his own son. Daniel was barred from entering his home.

The relationship was strained further when Johann Bernoulli published "Hydraulica," a piece on hydrodynamics, in 1739, while Daniel published "Hydrodynamica," a very similar work, one year earlier. Johann blatantly plagiarised "Hydrodynamica" in his publication of "Hydraulica," and even backdated it to 1732 to make it appear as if his work was first published. However, the plagiarism was quickly discovered.

Daniel Bernoulli continued to work in calculus, mechanics, energy conservation, oscillation, and the movement of bodies in a resisting medium in his later years. He belonged to the Royal Society of London, the Paris Academy of Sciences, the Berlin Academy, and the St. Petersburg Academy of Sciences. Bernoulli died on March 17, 1782, in Basel, just a few weeks before his eighty-second birthday
